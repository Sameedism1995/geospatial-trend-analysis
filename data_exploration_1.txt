Exploratory Data Analysis
Topic: ML-Based Geospatial Analysis of Coastal Water Quality and Urban Exposure

	•	Introduction and Data Description
This report presents the exploratory data analysis conducted on datasets collected to study the relationship between maritime shipping activity and coastal water quality in the Åland Sea and Turku Archipelago. The study area covers the sea region between longitude 19.5–22.5° East and latitude 59.7–60.7° North, an area known for heavy ferry and cargo traffic connecting Sweden and Finland through the Åland Islands.

Three datasets were collected and merged for this study.

	•	Sentinel-2 Satellite:
The first dataset consists of water quality observations derived from Sentinel-2 satellite imagery for the year 2023. These observations include three spectral indices commonly used in remote sensing of water bodies:

	•	Normalized Difference Chlorophyll Index (NDCI) which estimates chlorophyll-a concentration in surface water and serves as an indicator of algal growth and eutrophication.
	•	Normalized Difference Turbidity Index (NDTI) which captures water murkiness caused by suspended particles such as sediment, organic matter, or pollutants.
	•	Normalized Difference Water Index (NDWI) which provides a broader measure of water content and overall surface water condition.

	•	European Marine Observation and Data Network (EMODnet):
The second dataset comes from the European Marine Observation and Data Network (EMODnet) and provides vessel traffic density, measured in hours per square kilometer, averaged over the period 2017–2023. The dataset distinguishes between four vessel categories:
	•	Cargo ships
	•	Passenger ferries
	•	Tankers
	•	Fishing vessels

It also includes a combined total vessel density variable representing all vessel types.

	•	HELCOM HOLAS 3 Chlorophyll-a:
The third dataset is the HELCOM HOLAS 3 chlorophyll-a assessment, which contains ecological quality assessments for water bodies across the Baltic Sea based on in-situ laboratory measurements collected between 2016 and 2021. This dataset serves as independent ground-truth validation data for the satellite-derived observations.
After spatial joining through a nearest-neighbor approach, the merged master dataset contains 1,302 observations and 37 variables, covering 10 months of the year 2023 across the full study area.



	•	Understanding the Water Quality Variables
Before examining the relationship between shipping and water quality, it is important to understand the basic statistical characteristics of the three water quality indicators.

	•	NDCI Distribution:
NDCI values range from −1.0 to 0.494, with a mean of −0.085. Most values cluster within a narrow range between −0.11 and −0.02, but the distribution contains a long tail of extreme negative values.
The distribution is heavily left-skewed, with:
	•	Skewness: −2.602
	•	Kurtosis: 13.927

A Shapiro–Wilk test confirms that the data is not normally distributed (p < 0.001).

The negative mean indicates relatively low chlorophyll concentrations across most of the study area, consistent with the oligotrophic to mesotrophic conditions typical of the Åland Sea. However, the extreme negative values may reflect cloud shadows, land interference, or sensor artifacts, which should be considered during later analysis.

	•	NDTI Distribution
NDTI values range from −1.0 to 0.024, with:
	•	Mean: −0.307
	•	Standard deviation: 0.147
The distribution is also left-skewed, though less strongly than NDCI:
	•	Skewness: −1.134
	•	Kurtosis: 4.232

The consistently negative values indicate that the waters in the study area are generally clear, which aligns with expectations for this region of the Baltic Sea where suspended sediment loads are relatively low.

	•	NDWI Distribution
NDWI has the most balanced distribution among the three indicators.
	•	Range: 0.001 to 1.0
	•	Mean: 0.501
	•	Skewness: −0.065
The distribution is nearly symmetric, and values are spread relatively evenly across the range. This behavior is expected because NDWI measures general water surface properties, which tend to vary gradually across space and time.

4. Seasonal and Spatial Patterns
Water quality indicators exhibit strong seasonal variation.
Monthly averages of NDCI, NDTI, and NDWI change considerably across the year due to environmental processes typical of the Baltic Sea:
	•	Spring: nutrient runoff from snowmelt
	•	Summer: increased temperature and sunlight driving algal blooms
	•	Autumn: reduced light and vertical mixing of the water column
These natural cycles account for much of the variation in the water quality data and must be considered when evaluating the influence of shipping.







Spatial Variation
Water quality also varies geographically.
The eastern part of the study area, closer to the Turku Archipelago, shows different water characteristics compared to the western open waters of the Åland Sea. Coastal waters tend to be:
	•	Shallower
	•	More enclosed
	•	More influenced by land-based nutrient inputs.
	•	Distance from Ports
	•	Most observations occur far from major ports.

7. Summary of Observations
Several key insights emerge from the exploratory analysis:
	•	Water quality indicators are non-normally distributed and exhibit strong seasonal variation.
	•	Vessel density data is highly zero-inflated and contains many outliers corresponding to shipping lanes.
	•	Simple high-versus-low traffic comparisons do not reveal significant differences in water quality.
	•	When spatial factors are considered, particularly distance from ports, strong statistical relationships appear.
	•	Tanker traffic shows the most consistent association with water quality indicators.
	•	HELCOM assessments confirm that water bodies in the study area do not meet environmental quality targets, providing broader context for the satellite observations.
