I followed up with an additional exploratory analysis focused on your main point: whether we can observe systematic differences between main sea-lanes and low-traffic areas (and whether any signal decays with distance from the sea-lanes). 1) Sea-lanes vs (almost) no-traffic areas, and distance-to-lane gradients
How “sea-lanes” were defined (practical, non-causal exposure proxy)
To approximate the main shipping corridors, I used the EMODnet vessel density grid (2017–2023 averaged). I classified a point as “sea-lane” if its nearest density grid cell belongs to the top 10% of vessel density (density_total_log ≥ 0.4496; q90). This yields:
	•	Sea-lane points: 129 
	•	Background points: 1,511 
	•	Total Sentinel-2 water samples (2023): 1,640 
I also computed the distance from each Sentinel-2 sample to the nearest “sea-lane” grid cell and produced distance-decay plots (median and IQR by 5 km bins).
Indicators examined
To stay close to shipping-plausible pathways, I focused on optical indicators related to suspended matter / turbidity and surface anomalies:
	•	NDTI (turbidity proxy) 
	•	red/green ratio (B4/B3; alternative suspended matter proxy) 
	•	FAI (surface reflectance anomaly proxy) 
NDCI was kept only as background context and is not interpreted as shipping-driven.
Sea-lane vs background comparisons (non-parametric)
Because distributions are non-normal, I used Mann–Whitney U tests.
All points (q90 lane definition):
	•	NDTI: median(lane) = −0.3314 vs median(background) = −0.2945, p = 0.016 
	•	red/green ratio: median(lane) = 0.5022 vs median(background) = 0.5459, p = 0.0166 
	•	FAI: no significant difference (p = 0.485) 
Restricted comparisons to reduce obvious confounding
To reduce the risk that “lane vs background” is simply separating open sea from near-coastal/archipelago waters, I repeated the same tests with restrictions:
Only points 10–50 km from nearest port (N = 959):
	•	NDTI: median(lane) = −0.3391 vs median(background) = −0.2764, p = 0.00181 
	•	red/green ratio: median(lane) = 0.4936 vs median(background) = 0.5676, p = 0.00193 
	•	FAI: no significant difference (p = 0.875) 
Only points 20–50 km from nearest port (N = 830):
	•	NDTI: median(lane) = −0.3314 vs median(background) = −0.2772, p = 0.0182 
	•	red/green ratio: median(lane) = 0.5022 vs median(background) = 0.5673, p = 0.0169 
	•	FAI: no significant difference (p = 0.627) 
Only points with low SWIR reflectance (B11 ≤ 0.05) to reduce mixed pixels (N = 1,577):
	•	NDTI: median(lane) = −0.3363 vs median(background) = −0.2998, p = 0.0116 
	•	red/green ratio: median(lane) = 0.4985 vs median(background) = 0.5417, p = 0.0121 
	•	FAI: no significant difference (p = 0.623) 
Taken together, this suggests that (at least for 2023 and within this study area) turbidity-related optical indicators show consistent lane/background differences under several reasonable restrictions, whereas FAI does not separate the two groups in the current setup.
Direction of the difference (important)
In these comparisons, the lane medians are lower than the background medians for NDTI and the red/green ratio. I do not interpret this as “shipping improves water quality” or “shipping worsens water quality”; rather it indicates that lane and background areas have systematically different optical characteristics. This may reflect a combination of factors such as open-sea vs archipelago water types, bathymetry, coastal influence, and local hydrodynamics. Therefore, the lane/background results are best treated as an empirical spatial contrast rather than causal evidence.
